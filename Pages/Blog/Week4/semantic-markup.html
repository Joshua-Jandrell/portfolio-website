Sematic markup:
What is it? Who cares?

23 March 2022

By Joshua Jandrell

Sematic markup is one of the first buzz words to be thrown around when it comes to well-written html. It is the idea of giving elements useful tags which appropriately describe their function and meaning. Simplistically, it is the difference between storing this article in an <article> element as opposed to a <div>. This may have not impact on the visual styling of this page when viewed on your computer screen but it has a profound impact on how things are viewed by your computer. Good semantics allow computers, web crawlers and screen readers to get a better understanding of the intended function of each line of text.

Okay… So what does it mean to be semantic?

According to the Collins dictionary: (sɪmæntɪk) ADJECTIVE [usually ADJECTIVE noun] Semantic is used to describe things that deal with the meanings of words and sentences.” In short, semantics means meaning. Although, sematic meaning (although tautology) speaks specifically to the distinct and specific meaning of a symbol. (…or a word – but if you ask me worlds are linguistic symbols. I’ll just use symbol from now on.) What exactly does this symbol communicate? How does its meaning differ from similar symbols? Does a symbol have multiple meanings?  What are the implications and connotations of using this symbol? How could it be interpreted? It is the difference between brisk and fast, or = and ≈. Furthermore, these 2(meanings) are ∑ × ∫ puns puns. (Have fun with that one).

And what is markup?

According to the Economic times “Markup refers to the value that a player adds to the cost price of a product.” And so sematic markup would be the value markup adds to – hang on! We have a sematic issue here! My silly old web browser doesn’t understand language in the way that you or I do. The symbol ‘markup’ has different meanings in different contexts. How the heck is it meant to know which one I want?

Okay, after a more semantic search, Encyclopaedia Britannica told me that a markup language, is “a standard text-encoding system consisting of a set of symbols inserted in a text document to control its structure, formatting, or the relationship between its parts.” An example of this is HTML (Hypertext markup language) which has been used to organise the structure of the page you are on right now. Markup refers to the tags and labels used to tell your browser every bit of text is. Is it a heading? Is it a link to another web page? Is it this article you are reding now?

But I still don't care. Why should I?

Let’s Imagine I want to organise all my belongings into different labelled boxes. I could label the boxes “things” and “stuff”. These labels are technically correct, and the boxes will function as boxes, they might even look quite nifty. But you’d have no clue where to look if you wanted to find my bright green highlighter. Is it a ‘thing’ or a ‘stuff’? Now imagine if you were my silly search engine. You don’t know the difference between markup and markup, you have no clue what highlighter is. You can’t even see green, let alone speak English. This highlights (sorry, not sorry) the issues with un-sematic markup. Html tags like <div> and <span> mean little more than ‘things’ and ‘stuff’.

Combining the definitions above tells us that semantic markup means Meaningful labelling. It is about accurately and effectively communicating the structure, formatting, and relationship between parts of a document (like this web page). Fancy styles and formatting alone only imply meaning on your screen, but not to a machine.  It is much like me expecting you to find my highlighter in the green box of stuff with my pencil case sticking out – you’d have known if you could see it. Good markup will make a document more analysable and readable to a computerized visitor. The computer needs to see labels it understands. A box labelled “stationary” may be quite useful, and a bag labelled highlighters within that box would help too. This will improve the searchability of a web page. Furthermore, it will allow screen readers to give a better description of the webpage, enhancing accessibility and understanding. It’s just good practice to keep your ‘stuff’ well organised. Especially if you have all kinds of visitors and you expect them all to find things.

It is all to easy to see the internet as a visual medium. But if a pictures says a thousand words then sematic markup should say them too. That way everyone (human or computer) is on the same [web]page.
