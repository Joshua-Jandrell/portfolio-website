''As We May Think' at 65' at 12

10 March 2022

By Joshua Jandrell

A reletivly personal reflection on what we have done and where we have come as a society. How has exposure to technology changed the way in which we view technology? How should I while moving forward in a technical space.

Oh my hat! - this article is... quite something. It's a reflection on a reflection: longwinded, convoluted and one of the first I wrote. Reader discretion is advised.

What's going on?

The title is a mess - I know. I just can't help myself. What can one do other than revel in the 'metaness' (on afflation to Facebook) of the whole situation. Here I am writing a blog about and article about an article. The blog-ception (artiception sounds too much like an edible plant) of it all is just too beautiful. I love the concept of ideas building up on each other and thoughts being compounded. >I like to think this is how Bush imagined it, one article linking to the next allowing thoughts to organically grow. It is fitting that we continue to build on Bush's idea - appending our thoughts to his great trail: one of many strands in a giant, world-wide, web.

Any how I digress - you're probably wondering what the heck I'm on about.

What is 'As We May Think?

In July 1945 the Engineer and visionary, Vannevar Bush, published his (now famous) article As We May Think in the Atlantic. In this essay Bush discourses how he imagines technology progressing after the world war II, and how he feels it should progress. Bush predicts that Humanities' ability to accurately construct more complex mechanisms is bound to increase. This new-found power should be used to store all humanities knowledge in a more accessible manner. Bush goes on to propose a revolutionary concept: the Memex. This hypothetical contraption will store information on microfilm (far more compact than books - which are soon to be obsolete) but, its most profound feature is not how information is stored; rather how it is accessed. The Memex will not use a rigid index, for this is an abstract and arbitrary structure, incompatible with the human perception of ideas! Instead articles within the Memex will be linked to together in trials - defined by the user. These paths will store documents based other how the user associates them mimicking links in the brain. Each article can be part of several trails forming a great web of linked information. In this web the the user will able to instantly jump from one article to another, following the users seemingly random trains of thought.

What is ''As We May Think at 65''

In 'Spring' (whatever that means on a planet with two hemispheres) 2010, some 65 years later, the computer scientist and researcher, Simon Harper (who is impossible to find on Wikipedia because us foolish humans still index information with words) published his article "As We May Think At 65" in the SIGWEB newsletter. Harper points out that even after 65 years the thoughts of Bush are poignant. Many pioneers in the field of hypertext and the world wide web sight 'As We May Think' as their inspiration. Although, while profoundly influential, Harper feels Bush's true vison remains unfulfilled. He notes that Bush remained devoted to the physical world and tangible experience. Bush spent his time constricting a complex mechanical integrator - refusing to embrace the abstract seeds of digital computation which were simultaneously being sown at his own institution. In a similar manner, Bush rejected the concept of abstract data/information hierarchies: he believed that information should be stored as associations within the human mind. Harper goes on to explain how Bush believed that, "the computer should adapt itself to accommodate human needs; not the reverse." In fact, Bush extended this notion to science itself. The key concept of "As We May Think" was not the fanciful designs of complex mechanism but rather the passionate belief that technology could be used to enhance the human experience and augment thought itself. This is the philosophy of the innovator and the inventor - a philosophy to which Harper (a man whose research focusses on enhancing the accessibility of technology) undoubtedly subscribes. Harper feels that the ideas of Bush have not demised over timer or chancing technology - the needs of the humanity remain.

And what's all this then?

And now, in March 2022, some 12 years after Harper (and 77 years after Bush) the Interactive Media student, Joshua Jandrell (who is now referring to himself in the third person) wrote this:

Much has changed since the time of Bush, and even since the time of Harper. Technologies rapid advancement has left once new terms like "web 2.0" sounding as ancient as "micofilm". The internet is more massive and more important to human life than Bush could ever have imagined. The world wide web has become far more than just a repository of knowledge, it hosts a far broader spectrum of humanity as a whole. Humans after all are social beings - so it is natural that much of the internet is devoted to socialization, entertainment an recreation. In this age data is at our finger tips, so easily accessed it has become mundane. Yet the echoes of Memex and Bush's vison still live on.

Before we continue I must confess that I am a mere flee; crawling across the shoulders of giants. I am so small in comparison that these shoulders are not lofty height to me; but rather a flat plain. A new 'ground' upon which to build. I know precious little about the birth of the internet and even less about the technology of the time before. You see I grew up with the internet: from playful childhood wonder of long-lost with flash games to my angsty teenhood of social media's explosion. I now live in a time where the world is a screen, the internet is like water, and the answer is google (some - but perhaps not all - connotations intended).

And what may I think?

Given my experiences, when I first read 'As We May Think' I was inclined to cast it aside as the stale vison of a man who had failed to move with the times. A doubtlessly great thinker, perceiving the future's vague shapes on the horizon; unable to see their true from through the mist of time. Bush's concept of an automated shop seems quaint in a world of barcodes and online banking. Even the fabled Memex is a frivolous though experiment, making some predictions but failing to capture the full picture of societies progress.  After all, Bush was fixated on the tangible and the physical when the world moved to the digital and the abstract. He had achieved true mastery of his mechanical realm but was afraid to move on to the next. In this new digital world he too would be a feel. Perhaps Harper intended to make similar criticisms when he mentioned the Differential Analyzer; ironically this is what shifted my paradigm about Bush, filling me with reverent awe.

There is no shortage of software capable of solving integral equations: I seldom marvel at this feat. In the abstract world of software, mathematical computation capacity is expected. After all, computers are built on Math. Yet I cannot begin to imagine how such a system could exist mechanically. The only from of analogue integration I can imagine is the op-amp integrator circuit. Still a piece of abstract electronic circuitry; overshadowed by Bush's physical device. I have begun to realize that Bush was a master of a long forgotten art. My psyche has been so shaped by technology that I've lost part of my connection to the physical. So much so that I could not conceive the machine Bush created.

Nor could I conceive the Memex, at least not in it's true form. (However, I doubt Bush could imagine memes so we are even!) While I may still scoff at the idea of microfilm, I am taken aback by the idea of indexing information by thought patterns of the human mind. This notion seemed chaotic and unreasonable to me - both in use and technical implementation. Even though my own chaotic ramblings here (fool of tangents and interjections) prove Bush's assertions about the human mind. I am so conditioned by the world of abstract technology that I have learnt to adapt to its capabilities. I have learnt to see the rigid and indexed form of the internet as a superior storage method (although search engines do make its navigation far simpler than that bush imagined).

I am a mere flee, navigating an ever-changing plain, adapting my path based on its obstacles.

...now back to As We May Think

In mamy respects Harper was correct to state the Bush's vision had not fully been realized. In fact in some respect the Memex was superior to the comparators of today. It could be operated entirely by speech - or perhaps even by thought, connecting directly to the brain. Such functionality would resolve the accessibility issues with the modern web which Harper is trying to address. Although in my mind Harpers comments cut far deeper than the form of technology, they address its function.

Bush's mechanical from of pistons and microfilm is long gone - ironically outlived by books. Yet his faith in science is more profound than ever. Bush and Harper's belief that technology should benefit humanity is not the whim of naïve inventors: it is a mission that should be followed by all. I have spent my life imagining what technology could do, and how it capabilities could impact humanity. As a future designers (and human general) it is my duty to focus on what technology should do and how humanities needs can shape it.

Bush was was not looking at the horizon: he was looking beyond it. He did not fail to predict the future; it failed to meet his vison.

What May I Do?

I cannot afford to be a flee crawling on the shoulders of giants. It is my duty to stand up and be a human, to survey the vast world below, to look beyond the horizon and to forge a new path.

Of course I must also be wary, like Bush one is likely only to imagine a fragment of what is to come. The functions and concepts of the Memex have more similarities with Wikipedia - an online encyclopedia (which I have been 'subtly' linking) - than the internet as a whole. Wikipedia form an online repository of free information. It allows users to edit articles and add links to related articles - these links follow any reasonable connection within the author's mind. Bush alone did not have dreams which could be realized by the world wide wed instead it is a collective, ever changing effort of humanities whole.

The key message of Harpers essay was not the longevity of Bush's Memex but rather the immortality of his vision to tailor technology for humanities good. Of course 'good' is a perspective. Yet, any design intended to benefit humanity is likely to hold value 22; 65 or even 77 year late. Even if its function has taken a very different from.

I will end with a quote of Harper quoting Bush, "Bush is quoted as saying that, ‘Science has a simple faith, which transcends utility. It is the
faith that it is the privilege of man to learn to understand, and that this is his mission.’ Personal associations derived from experience, and not abstract structures, enables us to learn
better and understand better, in this way Bush’s faith in science and humanities mission
can be better accomplished."
