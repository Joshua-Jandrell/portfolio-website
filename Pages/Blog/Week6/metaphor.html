What's all this Meta-phor?

9 April 2022

By Joshua Jandrell

So you're surfing the web and land on my site. You browse around before navigating to the blog page. Then you follow the link that takes you to this post. Or maybe this addressed popped up in your search results because a crawler picked this article for you. Either way you're here - so welcome. At this point in time you are either nodding in agreement or scratching your head in confusion - what exactly is going on? Shakespeare would probably rip his hair out over all the mixed-metaphors and conflicting imagery. Read the first paragraph again while imaging it outside of its digital digital context. What the heck is going on? Are you surfing a wave; crawling across a spider-web; navigating to a street address; or paging though a book? May be you are doing all of the above, or non of the above… It's no secret that we use copious amounts of metaphors when referring to the digital realm. But why do we use them? How do we choose them? What do they tell us? What don't they tell us? And, why is it worth keeping such a mess of confounding analogies.

What's a metaphor?

Ah, that's easy: a metaphor is a direct comparison. Metaphors are the strongest form of comparison - suggesting that the the concepts compared are not just similar but in fact equivalent to one another. Naturally this greatly highlights similarities between the compared ideas - simultaneously masking and down-playing the differences. Of course, as figurative langue metaphors are not literal: they can be vague or specific, vague or contextual. Two objects need not to be exactly (or even remotely) alike when compared via a metaphor. Metaphors often transfer connotations and denotations. Calling King Henry a lion suggests that he was a strong and brave leader rather than evoking any clear similarities between them an a predatory cat. I'm waffling on a bit but the point I wanted to make is that metaphors hold great deal of descriptive power and can be used to evoke a wide array of associations and connotations.

One more thing: metaphorical comparisons do not need to be explicitly stated, they can be implied. For example, if I were to tell you that I was "battening to meet deadlines" I would be implying a metaphor between the work I need to do and a violent and stressful conflict (all connotations and conditions apply). This sort of metaphorical language is particular prevalent in the the abstract digital context where metaphors are often implied or extended buy the way information is described, the manner in which tasks are conducted and even the names given to data structures and other artifacts.

But why so many digital metaphors?

How else would you describe an abstract world? At its core, digital information is pure data - nothing more than 1's and 0's. This raw data is formless, tasteless and [description missing]. It is hard for us humans to visualize or comprehend. We are better at recognizing patterns, making connections between ideas and applying prior knowledge to new satiations. Metaphors let us play to our strengths: explaining abstract and complex concepts in more familiar and finite terms.

A good example of this is abstract data types (Stacks, queues, heaps, tress, graphs etc.). Each 'type' is a means of organizing digital information in a manner that is intuitive for humans. The names of there types are effetely metaphors. For example let me tell you about the thingymabob. The first piece of data that goes into a thingymabob is the last piece of data that come out of it. This concept is not necessarily easy to understand or remember. But what if I told you that the thingymabob is a 'stack' of data. Now you have an intuitive mental understanding of what's going on. The first item in is the one on the bottom - the most recently added one is on the top. Obviously the top most item is the one that will be the simplest to remove. This analogy defines how the data is sored and manipulated and allows humans to think about the ADT in a taggable manner.

The term 'web' plays a similar role. This time describing how data is connected. Vannevar Bush [1] goes to great length to explain the vast and versatile links between information in his hypertext-inducing Memex. Bush eventually refers to this multitude of paths as a web of information. This metaphor is apt: describing threads of thoughts spanning between numerous points. Spider webs are organic and do not have a strict regimented structure (however they are still constructed with some logic and purpose). Bush hoped that the memex would give humans the same 'organic freedom' to flexibly position strands between ideas without the harsh rules of computerized indexing. The notion of the world wide web is a similar concept but on a global scale. Although not quit what bush imagined the internet (a net is just a man-made web with structure) is alive and well and crawling with spider-bots.

Other metaphors highlight different aspects of the web. For example the notion of webpages invokes the idea of a digital library. This makes sense: the internet is filled with articles and information - so is a library of books or an archive of information. You are reading on this webpage right now after all. This also invites the notion of digital informing storage. Although perhaps this description fails to capture the intricate and varied traversal created by the interwoven stands of hypertext searching for an article can be seen as looking though a library.

Perhaps this notion of navigating a physical space is better for describing internet traversal. Once you know a friends street address you can visit. Once you know the address of a website we can travel there. Most websites take you to a 'home' page which forms a landmark for further internet exploration. Think of 'web traffic' or 'visitors' to a site. This metaphor portrays the web as a public space and acknowledges that users can easily move from one point to another. It also makes abstract and unpredictable hypertext feel more approachable. hyperlinks are routs between different destinations Simon Shum [2] notes that understanding hypertext through the metaphor of a digital space can allow users to gain a better cognitive map of its structure. Moving from one site to another simplifies the user's understanding while providing a valuable means to unpack the users experience. Shum notes that much like physical spaces, digital spaces can have qualities, connotations and associations. Isn't this a great analogy! (It has it's moments, but digital spaces are non-Euclidian which is a bit of a bummer.) In fact the previous two were great analogies too! We have simplified the web with a metaphor and now we understand it!

I would be very surprised if anyone could describe the world wide web without using one of them (they couldn't even say the name). The three metaphors mentioned are just the tip of the iceberg. Imagine trying to describe any digital structure without using any metaphors at all. What is the initial landing space on your computer if not a desktop? (Don't be cleaver and say shell, that's just a metaphor for a machine's external layer.) What is a folder? What is this 'thing' you are on right now if not a site or a page.

Hang on a moment - You've lost your marbles. You just naming things! These aren't metaphors

Aren't they? Sure they are what we call 'digital stuff' but these 'names' represent physical objects. That's no coincidence. It would be almost impossible to understand the abstract concepts they represent without the more taggable affordances of their namesake items. And that is the problem!

The mind is Clouded by Metaphores

Our understanding of digital information is so defined by metaphorical mapping that our metaphors begin to define how we understand digital information. Entries in the stack ADT are not actually piled on top of each other - we just imagine that they are - data in the stack could be strewn all over physical memory. Similarly, you don't visit a website - it visits you. Your browser retrieves a document from a server and displays it on your screen (that library metaphor wasn't half bad). >

A metaphor is figurative langue. In a poetic device. It doesn't need to be accurate. It just needs to convey the intended message. Think about surfing the web - this metaphor was coined by Jean Armour Polly with the intent of making web browsing sound fun and adventurous [3]. Using the internet has as much to do with a costal watersport as a King Henry with a carnivorous feline. >

Whatever. If it makes sense, who cases what metaphors mean?

With metaphors defining the affordances we give digital information - their connotations are vital. Think of the cloud. A mysterious ethereal realm where one can backup all your data (saving it for a rainy day). On the surface this makes sense. the cloud is an ever present repository of information. It is as if your files exist in the atmosphere. In realty 'the cloud' is just a big old server. Like any computer it has limited space and high energy requirements, two feature that tend to be overcast by the cloud paradigm.

Digital metaphors are often political and push certain agendas either intentionally or by accident. Maria Lindh [4] observes how metaphors obscure the sociopolitical implications of digital mediums. When an archive server become a cloud it feels like a natural, almost mystical, phenomenon. Those using 'the cloud' become unaware of the technical infrastructure supporting its functionality or the true potential and implications of the assonated technology. In 'Metaphors in critical Internet and digital media studies' Sally Wayatt [5] adds that such metaphors can define how a digital artifact is used and designed. Terms like data mining and big data as new gold/oil present data as a natural resource which should be extracted for fanatical gain. This uncountably changes the way big data is used, collected an managed with a focus on maximizing profit.

Validating an NFT is a good technical description but it sound like a boring administrative process. Minting in the block-chain, on the other hand, evokes a comparison between NFTs and currency. And thus it is implied that NFTs must have some sort of inherent monetary value. Could this me a factor in the huge value assigned to NFTs by society?

Now you just yelling names and rambling about NFTs for some reason

No… okay maybe a little but I have a point. Seeing metaphors as mutual exclusive defections for digital artifacts obfuscates the true nature of the artifact. In 'The medium is the message' Marshall McLuhan mentions the far-reaching implantation of electric light as a medium of pure information. How much harder would such an observation have been if the lightbulb had been seen as a 'glass candle'. This highlights the similarities in function but fails to address (or even acknowledge) the impact of electrical power. Many of digital metaphors are glass candles:

  • The web-paradigm describes a structure of linked information (or devices) but does little to unpack what that information does. Webs are static while data changes and flows.
  • The library metaphor describes archived information but ignores the progression of the medium.
  • The digital space analogy is like the stake ADT, relying on conventional understanding and implied use rather than technical structure or functionality.
  • Surfing the web is harmlessly illogical.
  • The cloud-backup metaphor describes the utility of a digital while trying to pretend that it isn't a digital archive.
  • Data mining metaphors emphasize the importance of information whole commodifying personal data.
  • Minting in the block chain describes authentication while obfuscating process and implying inherent value.

All of these metaphor succeed in highlighting some features while burring others.

Ah I get it now. All these metaphors are bad right?

No Digital Metaphors are great because they define how we understand, view and use information.

A metaphor is a painting. It depicts its subject more succinctly than words could ever describe. It's powerfully descriptive: alive more emotion feeling than any honest photograph. There is nothing wrong with art. As long as we are aware that a painting is not realty.

Digital metaphors are artist tools for unpacking abstract information. They give an intuitive vision of what happening. But they are inaccurate and limited. Using these metaphors is to be encoring because it allows us to visualize what's going on. However, all these tool have limitation and should serve to augment understanding rather than replace it.

It is wonderful that there is a variety of digital metaphors. This gives us a more diverse set of lenses to view technology through. Each metaphor depicts the concept from a different angle and gives alternative insights. If anything, we should be upset that there aren't more digital metaphors. Who cares if our metaphors are mixed - they are not pieces of a puzzle,but fragments of a collage.
