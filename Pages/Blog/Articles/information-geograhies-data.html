(im)mutable augmentations

Information has always had geography. It is from somewhere; about somewhere; it evolves and is transformed somewhere; it is mediated by networks, infrastructures, and technologies: all of which exist in physical, material places.(Graham el at, 2015)

Graham et al seek to immediately establish that information is inevitably linked to geography and physical location. Information is held somewhere and for it to be transferred it must be moved somewhere else. However the geography of information is not merely limited to where it is stored. Information is influenced by the culture and context of the location it is created in. And, in turn the politics of an area are shaped by the information accessible within the area (and perhaps also the information about the area).

It is acknowledge that sometimes the location of information seems separate from the information itself. In the same way that an oral story can slowly be embellished with the the personal experience of each story-tell who passes it on, the geographic context of information can be over-written by each region in which it exists. As the location of information changes, its context changes, thus the manner in which the information is understood and transformed transformed changes. Information is altered and adapted when exposed to new contexts. However, according to Graham et al not that this is not always the case.

It is precisely this mobility and adaptability of information that provides the motivation to constrain the mutability of information through the creation of what Latour (1986) refers to as 'immutable mobiles' or information that can be transported without significant change to its inherent characteristics or meaning.

(Graham el at, 2015)

Graham et al view immutable mobiles s as essentially physical manifestations of information. They are tangible artifacts: books, newspapers, maps and pamphlets that are encoded with geographic information that cannot be overwritten by transportation. Fixing the information to a physical object allows it to be detached from its location. Although unaltered by travel, immutable mobiles is still completely bound by space and time - and are thus bound by geography.

But what about the internet? It appears to have transcended space. Surly it is not contained within a location or a physical artifact? The article addresses this next.

ICTs have also facilitated an evolution of information beyond immutable mobiles through the creation of '(im)mutable augmentations' characterized by the layering of dynamic information across and over geographic space. Thus, not only does information have particular geographies, but geography itself is layered, defined, and augmented by information that is more or less immutable(Graham el at, 2015)

Thoughts, opinions and ideas surrounding any location are themselves localized (not necessarily in the same location as they describe). Yet ICTs allow this information to be transmitted across the globe in an unaltered and immutable form. Information about a location comes form across the planet and information from a location is spread across the planet.

Is this not just globalization? The internet has given us the global village. Perhaps the location of information is now planet earth? No. Distinct cultural practices and ways of though still exist in different areas. These traditions and perspective have not suddenly become homogenous by simple being connected to one another. Despite how alarmingly uniform the internet may appear.

I feel that as similar argument could be made on a micro scale. Numerous schools of thought exist within a single geographical rejoin - country, province, city, town, building, room. The location of information matters - but so does its greater context. The personal experiences of an other might have a greater impact on their work than location in which they write. It cannot be debated that hegemonic mode of a location will shape its information however it is not the only factor. Graham et al acknowledge that more detailed and nuanced socio-economic issues should be investigated however, acquiring and displaying comparable data to describe this on a global scale is not an easy task. Geography may be one of many other factors impacting information but perhaps it is the easiest to measure.

The power of information

These (im)mutable augmentations of information about places matter because they shape how we are able to find and understand different parts of the world(Shelton et al. 2014)

Graham et al raise the concern that the immutable digital augmentations layers from one geographic location onto another are overwriting and superseding the exiting information of that location. Furthermore some geographic are so underrepresented that they might as well not exist. An underrepresented location is like restaurant which does not appear on a map - doomed to be ignore until it goes out of business and it completely and utterly forgotten.

Places invisible or discounted in representations are equally invisible in practice to many people. A restaurant omitted from a map can cease to be a restaurant if nobody finds it. Likewise, how places are presented within informational augmentations fundamentally affects how they are used or brought into being (Graham and Zook 2013)

Graham et al explain that information is power. If one controls the hegemonic narrative they wield power over the ways of though in an area. The article note that, prior to the digital era this control of information was relatively easy. Producing and engaging with immutable mobiles - such as printed books, maps or academic journals - was a privileges only afforded to a select few. The equipment, skill, finance an coordination required for the production of immutable mobiles was limited to large organizations such as governments and academic intuitions. Generally these intuitions (and their power) were located in the global North. There knowledge, bond to physical artifacts, was not slow moving and often contained in place of power. The South lacked the tools to contribute to this collection of information and were doomed to be spoken about rather than to speak. Graham et al wish to establish the historical backdrop with this point. The pre-digital distribution of knowledge and power have had a profound impact on how the world is shaped and, as the article argues, persist to this day.

The digital 'revulsion'

Graham et al explain that following the invention of ICTs, there was an idyllic optimism that this new technology would overcome the wrongs of the past and bring about an era of equal geographic access, representation and participation.

[f]or the first time in a millennium, we have a technology to equalize the opportunity that people have to access and participate in the construction of knowledge and culture, regardless of their geographic placingProfessor Lawrence Lessig, Harvard

According to the data provided in the article, this idea was a myth. The apparent omnipresence of the internet (in the global North) has made the world wide web an important battleground for political control. The means of production and distribution of information are less centralized and thus pollical ideals must be spread more violently. The internet is no a mere publication which can be censored; its (im)mutable augmentations must be challenged and overwritten more velociously. This is no longer the pursuit of large institutions but of each individual who wishes to make their opinion know. And these opinions and information are inevitably shaped by their location.

The article provides data to indicate that the global North is wield disproportionate power in the digital realm - with higher access, participation and representation. The South remain excluded, underrepresented and overwritten by northern information. Graham et al have established that because of the (im)mutable nature of the digital space, the deficit in southern contribution could lead to the overwriting and/or negation of information, cultures, politics and ideas in underrepresented rejoins. The North still hold hegemonic dominance and this is brining about a new epistemicide.

Establishing this context highlights the importance of the data which Graham et al are about to present. This preamble is of as much value as the data: unpacking the dangers and the stakes of digital colonialism and debunking the internet as a global medium of apolitical utility.

Now that the argument has been outline it is time to present data showing the geographic inequality of access, participation and representation.

Conclusion

in addition to uneven geographies of access to contemporary modes of communication, uneven geographies of participation and representation are also evident and are in some cases being amplified rather than alleviated.

As stated in the introduction Grahame et al conclude that inflation is still being produced predominantly in the global north. The internet has not brought about the board scale inclusion that had been hoped for. Spatial barriers remain key constraints for Who produces information is vital in the context of (im)mutable augmentations and power. The global south continues to be written about, forgotten and overwritten on the internet -it inhabitants remain voiceless.

In many ways “Towards a study of information geographies: (im)mutable augmentations and a mapping of the geographies of information” is ironic. There is not doubt that the article contains vital insights and its content should be taken seriously despite possible flaws in data collection. And yet it cannot avoid the irony of its circumstance an irony which is no fault of he authors but a cruel twist of fate. The article was published In Oxford and makes use of a Eurocentric academic framework. It is clearly written for academic purposes, a practice which it concedes generally remains limited to the global north. The article fundamentally excludes the global south - it is this meta narrative which drives Grahame et al's point home. It is another piece of information for the north about the south. The poor, voiceless disenfranchised south who required the aid of the great powerful north.

There are echoes of missionaries who ventured deep into 'darkest Africa' to 'civilize' lost souls. To 'educate' 'savages'. Some might have had sinister agendas but I truly thing that sad part is that these missionaries believed they were doing the right thing. They believed that the world needed their knowledge to survive.

I fear that although such acts are now regretted and frowned up, the modern solution is the digital savior. When it was once pants, region and books it is now Wi-Fi science and the internet.

None of these things have to be colonial but the power-dynamic in which they exist makes them so. If the internet is seen as utilitarian salvation and true equality it is doomed to overwrite exiting cultures and ways of though once more. the center of the 'world' wide web is America - Europe is its moon - Africa is barley an meteor, doomed to disintegrate in its atmosphere.

Graham et al are aware of this danger which is why they conduced this reperch and published this paper to inform others. The debunked the myth on the apolitical and no geographic internet and highlighted that historic divides persist. They even they appeared to hold a deep set belief that this is what the internet should aspire to become. I agree.

I fear this cannot be achieved with random random expansion and imposition. One must understand that up to this point digital technology has been built for northern needs on norther ideas. The internet is itself an immutable mobile and thus holds immense power.

Graham et al feel that future research should be done to investigate how an why each of these inequalities occurred and how they may be rectified. They also feel that research should be done to interrogate digital inequalities beyond representation.

I feel that along with the above more research should be done into creating and internet for the global south - and infract for each and every school of though possible. This should not be a webpage or artifact that simply slips into the current norther framework. It should not be a parody of what is already there. My hope is that the social and cultural needs of each rejoin can be understood and a digital system could be constructed that would enhance these features rather than overwriting them.

It is evident that many people are excluded from participation in the current system and research should be done into ensuring the web adapts to be more approachable t the entire world and not just English speaking former colonies.

More research should also be conduced into trends over time. Graham et al allude to to the trajectories of the data by do not explicitly unpack of display them. Static images are useful but dynamic mappings give a more coherent idea of how power is shifting. It is important to map any system with multiple feedback loops over time in order to analyze it effectively. Perhaps all of the concerns raised above are foolish as the world is moving to a more inclusive. perhaps the lack of participation has been exacerbated. Th data in this paper should be plotted regularly so that the nature of these trends may be observed

The key message of Graham et al in this text is a call to action. A call to get together as a human collective and figure out how a truly world word web could exist. More research should be done into how this is possible, if it is possible, how its creation should be managed and weather or not the internet should exist globally at all - with a particular focus on new invocative alternatives.
