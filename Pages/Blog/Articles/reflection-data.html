But html isn't programming: It's markup, which appears to mean the opposite of all that. Each time you want to do anything you have to make a detailed not about it. And if you want any sort of versatile navigation you need to make a reference to that note in 500 other places.

Well yup. That's kinda the whole idea of hypertext markup…

I over scoped

I added a bunch of things which I though would be trivial without understanding how they world work together. Html isn't the problem - It's me. When I design a system in other programming languages I think in code. I can see it all in my head. It feels logical, the system informs the design. I don't understand html well enough to do this yet. As my homepage suggests, I'm still printing 'hello world.

I'm a novice but I wanted by site to be extradentary. I had stary eyes and visons of gravure. I wanted to wow visitors with polished CSS and an solid structure. I wanted to show off skills I didn't have.

It is all to easy to see the internet as a visual medium. But if a pictures says a thousand words then sematic markup should say them too. That way everyone (human or computer) is on the same [web]page.

While programming the site I spent hours, not days, on non-essential features while the main structure didn't exist. I would faff about how clicking on a link felt even though it just led to error 404. I had my priorities mixed up. I was so obsessed with that extra ten percent that would give the site the x-factor that I forgot the 90 percent that would make the site work.

I'm angry with html because it can't do what I should only be doing later with JavaScript. I should accept that some plans can be implemented later. I should say “not now” but I can't. And that's my biggest failure.

I don't know how to say, 'good enough.'

Everything I have put on this site has a justification behind it. Goodness knows I won't have time to explain it all and you won't have time to read it. But trust me - it does.

I have spent countless hours deliberating. Adding things and removing remobing them. Not to and countless more faffing with CSS. This is why I feel like I work so slowly and that I can't get anything done.

The extra time taken, and extra work required never seem to be valid justification to me. I dismiss such concerns as laziness and insist to keep working to make things right.

I have ploughed relentlessly through the night, unable to sleep, even during load shedding, because I feel there is more work that must be done. That isn't healthy and this site isn't looking much better…

The worst part is when I look back at it all I didn't even address the things that truly mattered. I screwed around with stuff that was find, even though there are still serious issues which need to be resolved (like this refection).

The urge to keep working is irrational and illogic. It is not directed to constructive progress (even if I tell myself, it is). It is work for the sake of work, because nothing will ever be perfect, and I need to learn to accept that and move on.

No time management

I never had a timeline or any millstones when working on the site. I had a few loose once but I never stuck to them because they were never formalised. This lead to the task simply expanding to fill the time allocated. With neither the task nor the time clearly defined, this expansion has engulfed my existence.

This is not healthy and I need to protect myself against it moving forward. This is no the first time I've got to a state like this, but I need to do something about it.

From now on the work on this site will be governed by clear, time bound milestones. One the delaine for a section has passed I stop working on it (unless there is a glaring flaw). Sure, it won't be perfect. But nor is the site in its current state. If I'd had better time management I'd probably be polishing instead of panicking (or better yet sleeping).

Anyhow I've rambled on about my poor time management and impulsive work practices for long enough. Let's take a look at the site itself. I will not be reexplaining the intentions I described in the dev-log because this is tedious and redundant. I will be discussing wither or not the site is functioning as intended.

Evaluating the styling

I am pleased with the orange and blue colour scheme. The two shades I have chosen are working well together. These contrasting colures make each other pop rather than clashing and the site looks better than I had imagined in them. They give the index (and the entire site) the sense of enthusiasm and balance I had intended while still feeling joyful and peaceful. I did have to limit it to one hue of each colour to ensure the pallet did not feel too chaotic.

I did not execute my original concept of minimalism very effectively. I used a lot more decorative shapes and lines than I had initially intended. The site is abstract but perhaps not minimal. I enjoy the sense of structure and variety that these elements add. I am especially pleased with the time-line motif of the dev-log. It's aesthetically pleasing and gives the site more presence. The strong bold lines and grid layouts convey a sense of purpose drive and confidence.

I am also pleased with the square brackets and commented sub-headings. I find both to be visually appealing and they evoke the idea of programming and writing code (which is something I like to do). I do feel the heading decorations can be overused at times: I should think carefully about how frequently I use them so that they remain interesting and don't become overused or stale.

The monospace font can be a bit jarring; I am please I did not use it for body text. It is not the most aesthetically pleasing - but it also speaks to programming so and fits the theme well.

I don't know why but I don't like the navigation bar. Something feels off about it but I can't put my finger on it. It's too light and floaty compared to the big bold section heeding. I probably just need to play around with the styling, but that's a task for another day…

Evaluating the structure

I think that putting all of the section on the home page works out well. Everting is in once place for the visitor and they no-loner have the onus of finding section themselves (although they still can if they want to).

The 'Hello World' introduction section is impactful, but it doesn't feel like it belongs with the rest of the site. It was one of those impressive bits of polish I added for the sake of being impressive. Sure it looks cool but I may also confuse visitors who expect to land on a more traditional site. The best way to determine this sections future will be user testing. (If it try to decide myself I will just think in circles).

The about section feels more cluttered than the other sections despite not being to text heavy. It may help to add a solid boarder somewhere to reinforce the concept of structure which is more prominent elsewhere in the site.

I'm happy with the structure of the other index sections, although the games and explore portions feel rather bland There isn't much going on with them and its hardly exciting. The dev-log section is quite busy but looks well organised. The contact is probably my favourite: it is slick, minimal and does the job.

I need to re-evaluate how the blog section is presented. The current format is great for one or two blog posts but may be problematic once I start adding more.

The blog page layout is fine - pretty standard nothing inventive or extraordinary. The back button seems out of place wherever I put it. The dev-log back button has a similar issue, this feature needs a more consistent and appropriate section.

I think that the timeline like display in the dev-log section turned out great. It look interesting, conveys meaning and provides structure to the page. However, the index section for the dev-log is long, cumbersome and cluttered already. Using a continuous document may not be the best approach. The dev-log html is already unwieldly to manage; the sheer size of the page could increase loading time and effect performance. I'm also concerned about the <details> and <summary> widgets on this page. It is not immediately clear that each heading will expand to revied the full article. The block of text used to summarise the article might be confused for the article itself. Furthermore, the widget has no CSS transition which makes it hard for a suer to see the change they cased. I sometimes don't notice when I have opened a dev log article. I can style this to be more clear, but that might add clutter and look messy.

The games pages are my favourite. I like how they look and I like how it feels to hover over the downloads and what the square brackets expand. I was concerned about using orange boarder for the images in this section, but it worked out well and thinks don't seem to clash too much. My only gripe is that the back (“return”) button isn't where I said it would be on the wireframe. I have been very indecisive about the placement of this element and hardly feel this break the design. However, it is not consistent with the placement on the other pages which may cause confusion.

Reflecting on hypertext

Hyperlinks are generally displayed clearly. They are wither underlined or implied by being placed in a navigation element. The feedback given when a mouse hovering over a hyperlink is generally good. The use CSS transitions makes hovering over hypertext feel exciting and interactive with the page almost coming to life. That said - the communication for hypertext is not consistent, even within the navigation sections. I think I should define a clear styling convention and mouse hover behaviour moving forward.

The pop-out text used in the about section is a failure. I was intended to create flexible and expandable passages of hypertext and make reading feel like and act of discovery. (It would also allow me to add asides like this one without breaking the flow). Unfortunately, it looks messy and feels clunky. A clickable inline anchor is conventionally a hyperlink - that is what the user expect the pop-out text to be. I tried giving these a district colour and decoration however their functionality s still unclear. Having text pop out on hover could be a compromise but I will need too find a way to make this functionality accessible to screen reader users.

No testing for screen resizing

Because I have been faffing so much I have not tested this website on other devices with different screen sizes. I make extensive use for CSS grid elements and I'm not sure how these will look on different browsers. I am aware that things on my site start looking weird once the view with drop below 700px. This is what I will do next if I have time after setting up all the hyperlinks.
