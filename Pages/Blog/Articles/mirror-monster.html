Pages/Blog/Pages/mirror-monster.html The Internet: a monster or a mirror? 4 June 2022
From unequal access [1] to racist algorithms [2], it's hared to deny that the internet is riddled with danger, unfairness and inequality. By why? Is the interment an abomination: poorly connived and irresponsibly designed. Or does it merely reflect the injustices of society at large? Is the internet a monster - hard-coded with implicit braise - or a mirror - reflecting the injustice of society at large. Understanding what's wrong with the internet is great and all - but it's also high time we discussed how we can fix it

What? The internet is Amazing!

The power and utility of the internet cannot be denied! The internet has the potential to connect people across the world and facilitate a the sharing of divers experiences and ideas [3, 4]. This powerful tool can transcend space and time - allowing for global communication knowledge sharing and commerce [3]. The world wide web makes information accessible to all, improving access to knowledge and education [4].

… In theory yes - but in practice?

Not really. yes, the internet has the potential to be all of the above. But is doomed to fail. Internet access is not evenly distributed [1 ,4, 5] and a a result the internet is hardly a beacon of all encompassing human knowledge. Only about 59% of the world population has internet access [4] - but more disturbingly most of those who access, engage with and contribute to the internet are from the global north [1]. If the internet is to fulfil is promise of global utility and diversity allowing equal access must be a priority of digital justice [3,4]. Those without the interment will remain left behind, and forgotten [1, 3, 4] making digital representation a humanitarian issue and a basic human right [4]. If work is not done to provide internet access to all, exiting societal inequalities will be reflected and exacerbated in the digital space.

We made a monster.

But digital justice isn't really that simple. Aside from the logistical and concerns of providing internet to all; the internet in it's current form might not be as helpful as we think. We often see the internet (and technology as a whole) as proving solutions [5, 6] and answering problems.

'Tech == good' people are better with tech and the problem is that more people need tech.

This utility argument paints technology as a neutral tool. A benevolent medium which acts as an unbiased mirror - reflecting the injustices of society at large. As a result we often overlook its shortcoming as a lack of representation or un-just data [6]. How could this all be the fault of the internet?

The internet isn't just here and now: is a structure of data and a repository of information [7]. It's not just this web page - it's the data behind this web page, the code that made this web page. It's the algorithm that gave you this web page and the data you give it by visiting this web page. It the analytics used to profile you based on visiting this web page. It's all the pages you have ever vistited before - all the pages that have ever exited before. All the data that's associated with all that and all the data that existed before web pages were even a thing. It's big. It's scary. It's deep. It's dark and it's far more than chat rooms can cat memes.

Most Contributions to the internet (algorithms and cat memes alike) disporopotiinaly represent certain demographics and thus marginalize others [1, 5].Like any system with feedback - the sate of the internet is a function of time. The colonizing  positive feedback loop of northern dominance cannot be be undone with flash-in-the-pan diversity. (The classic example of this is a bath tub, filled with boiling water - one cannot suddenly open the cold tap an hop in expecting not to get burnt.) Once previously underrepresented people/cultures/ideas are suddenly exposed to a dominants digital couture: they risk being overwritten and lost [1, 4]] rather than embraced.

But why does it matter?

Okay sure. The internet was made by Americans [1] - but we all all use it now. Avoiding all technology is ludacris. Besides, this tub is far too full for us to pull the plug.  Perhaps we should just accept that cultures change. Society changes. Progress is inevitable. If you think that's bad you should hurl your device across the room, stop bathing and run around with a club. So there you have it folk. Digital digital justice is pointless. Who cares if some ideas are lot - we weren't using them anyway.

Well, progress is inevitable but, marginalization does not have to be. If technology were apolitical and unbiased than there would be no problem - but this line of though IS the problem. Algorithms are seen to be mirrors - reflecting societal data. But that mirror is convex! It's reflection is distorted - we see monsters as a result. Algorithms don't just reflect societal injustice in a data set - they perpetuate it [6]. They may be designed as unbiased and objective [2, 6] yet they cannot transcend the limited perspectives of their creators [5, 6]. As software developers it is all to easy to blame the data instead of question the implicit biased within out own solution [6]. Perhaps it's hard to see. Like drops of water going into a bath tub. But suddenly you get an algorithm which appears to have been coded to crops the elderly, Africans and Muslims out of photographs [2]. That's one of may, may examples [6]. It's all very well to identify and address these issues as they emerge. Calls to review problematic algorithms [2] and engage with civil society [5, 6] are important steps. But I refuse to believe that people with technical skills are unable to address these issues during development. Even if problems cant' fully be solved and trade-offs have to be made, I get it, there are no right answers. But at least think about it.

You don't need the internet; the internet needs you

Look, accidents happen. I have to concede that if I were to consider all of the thinks that could go and try my best to make an unbiased system I would fail. Something would go horribly wrong there would be unforeseen consequences. (well duh, that's what makes them unforeseen). As such, just, impartial software is impossible. And yet is is still an idea I believe we should strive towards.

Its all well and good to say that all humans need the internet's information [3, 4], but really the internet needs the perspectives of a diver society. Each new perspective gives new insights into the types of issues that could occur and the types of problems that need to considered. Each voice (or silent user's data trail) on the internet provides more data. All of these parts can only ever work together to make a greater whole.

So you, whoever you are, whatever you think. Don't ever be fooled into thinking that the interment is a benevolent provider. Nature or nurture, monster or mirror, the internet is only ever as good as it's users, its creators and the data it contains. It is our duty to be mindful of what that is and how we contribute to is - so that one day the utilitarian internet of equal access and divers participation may become a tangible idea rather than a misguided fallacy. To put more than just hot water in that bath tub so that in time more people feel comfortable to hop in.
