    The contact section

    This is the final section on my website. Once the visitor has seen all there is to see they know enough about be to decide if the want to chat. This section should be simple and easy to use.

I do not have linked in, twitter or face book (yet) - so no point in linking there. My Instagram and Pinterest accounts aren't applicable (and aren't in use much anyway).

    Developing this log

    After the blog section is the dev-log (development log) which keeps record of all the trials and tribulations while making the site. It will included styles guides, wireframes, reflections and well… you know because you're here.

    Dev-log? What? Why?

    It's basically a design journal for this site. Documenting the website design process helps me design and show that I am a thoughtful designer. It allows visitors (and myself) to understand what I was doing and possibly learn from my successes and mistakes.

    How will the dev-log section be structured?


    Similar structure to blog because it has similar features: periodic entries.

    List and featured articles swap position for visual variety, making the dev-log feel distinct and separate from the blog.

    The scroll list entri will be displayed as a time-line to show that develoment is a continous process with icremental installments the build on each-other.

    How will dev-log entires be structured?


    Unlike blog the dev-log is one continues document.

    WHY?

    Because this shows that development is a continuous process and that or articles are related and follow chronologically. In contrast each blog post convers a different topic.

    The timeline motif will be in the main dev-log section to show that it is a cautious process. This also indicates that entireties build on what has already be done.

    Sections in the dev-log will be collapsed using the <details> and <summary> widget so that users can scroll through the log more easily.

    I am slightly concerned that with time a single continuous document of this nature will become difficult to manage and slow to load. I may need to add some sort of document fetching system using JS.

    Blog structure

    How should the blog section and blog posts be structured?

    Games will be followed by a blog section. Visitor's know what I do but now they get to see who I am. The blogs should feel conversational and I'll try to make them a bit humorous.

    Why should the blogs be informal?

    I want to give the impression that the blogs are not censored or formalised. Using casual language like contraction shows that that the blogs aren't meant to be overly academic or formal. The deformalized context makes them feel more genuine. This section gives visitors an idea of who I am and how I think. I'm not going to give any false pretences.

    That said, I'm also not going to be an idol. This is the internet: putting too much of myself out there is dangerous and might come to bite me. I need to set boundary. I won't share everything or pour my heart out. I'll give my genuine opinions, but not all my opinions. I'll avoid swearing (event though everyone f@king does it) and try not to make and radically contentious claims. I must bare in mind that I am a brand and I can't bring my future employer's name into disrepute.

    I know this approach is a bit of a cop-out. Perhaps as I get more confident about being online I'll get bolder. This is still quite scary for me: I don't even use social media.

    Structure of the Blog section


    Recent blogs are most prominent, displayed in large blocks to the left - a natural starting point for the English-reader's eye. New work emphasised here because it is likely better and more topical. A short blurb is porvided for each post to get the visitor interested to read more.

    There is a list of all blogs is placed in a scroll space to the right for easy reference. This shows viewer there is more to read if they want. As the blog gets bigger I might sort blog entries into lists based on category and have a more comprehensive display of blogs on a seperate page. I may also add a search feature in further. For now this should be fine.

    Splash text (will eventually be randomised) is placed below the blog title. This sets a conversational tone and makes things fun. Blog section should feel friendly and inviting.

    Structure of the blog pages.



    Blog index gives can get a sense of the article structure. This is particularly helpful for screen reader users.

    Back button next to title, allows the user to return to main blog section if they wish (can be tweaked to send them to previous paged using JS later).

    The related blog sections are included to enrage the visitor to keep reading. They are paced on the side and agian at the bottom. The bottom ones will be in the same preview format that is used on the homepage. when the reader reaches the bottom of the page they are finished with the blog - sample posts should help to get them interested in something else.

    Share icons will allow the user to share the url over their social media. The url for this can be generated from the following site: Share Link Generator: Facebook, Twitter, LinkedIn, Pinterest, and Email mailto forms. This is super useful!

    Post coding notes

    Removed the right-hand related articles tab: this was redundant clutter. The reader will only need to look for more to read when they are finished reading (at the end of the page) If I want to refer to another article during the blog I can do so with a hyperlink.

    The footer section of the blog is stylistically distinct so that the reader can clearly see they have finished reading.

    Structure for game pages

    Each game will have a short article written about it to provide more information about what's going on before the visitor starts playing. This context will help them understand what the game is trying to do.

    How will this be structured?


    A short description about the game is very important because many of my games were made with limited time and I wasn't able to do everything I wanted. I need to explain what I was trying to do and what I was able to do. I will try keep this frank, breif and to the point so that visitors can get on with playing. This section is placed above the download section to encourage the viewer to read about my game first and understand the context before they play it.

    However, the download section should be prominent and distinctly formatted; making easy for one to download and play the game. That's what they are here for after all.

    This is followed by a credits section. Giving credits to myself and acknowledging the contribution of others is simply good practice - especially on a portfolio site. This may also allow me to link to my colleagues' websites in the future which is pretty cool!

    Back button encourages the viewer to easily return to the homepage and engage with more content.

    Share and feedback buttons are to encourage further engagement. I'd love my games to get exposure or learn how I could improve If the viewer does either its very beneficial to me. These are at the bottom because I assume most users would like to experience the content before they wish to talk about it.

    Images of gameplay will be placed down the right-hand side to give the visitor a preview of what's in the game without being intrusive or distracting. I considered videos but I'd rather people played my games to experience them for themselves.

    What happens when you land on the homepage?

    Taking a look at how a visitor may navigate though the site and finding ways to enhance that experience.

    What is important about the homepage?

    The homepage has to make a good impression. It is likely the first (and possibly only) thing with visitors to my site will see.

    It needs to amaze them, interest them and make them want to see more! I may not have long to get a visitators attention. Visors must be enthralled but they should be able to immediately know what I do when they glace at my webpage.

    How will you do this?

    I have a couple tricks up my sleeve…

    'Hello World.'

    That will be the first message visitors see when they land on my site. I know it's quite cliched, however it says a lot. When learning to program in a new language, printing 'hello world' is one of the first programs one writes. Opening with 'hello world' immediately tells the visitor - 'Hey I'm a programmer. I made this.

    "Hellow World" is also a greeting to the world. I'm acknowledging that I have just gone live on the internet for the first time. And that's pretty cool! This grounds also grounds me: it acknowledges that I have just begun my journey as a developer and still need to lean and grow while alos commemerating that fact that I am live on the wrold wide web.

    The visitor is just greeted by a back screen with 'Hello world' on it. It should feel like the command consol. Empty and blank. A monospace font is used to enhance this effect.


    And then what?


    They then scroll down and the site is revealed. The emergence of a bright and polished sight should have a significant impact. Everything will look brighter and slicker in contrast to the first back screen. This is to make and impactful first impression and convey the idea of personal growth.

    A rounded san-serif font will be used to enhance this contrast.

    Just Keep Scrowling

    As the visitor scrolls they unveil the site. In due course JavaScript will help me make this experience feel exciting and polished. This will transform the mundane act of scrolling down a webpage into an adventure. As the viewer scrolls down the site they become curios to see how things will change and what will come next. While scrolling, I introduce myself and I invite the visitor to look around the site. I don't necessarily expect them to follow my prompts. I just want them to keep scrolling.

    Once hey have started so they'll probably continue. I don't expect victors to explore my whole site (even if I would like them to) they'll probably just look at the homepage. Following hyperlinks implies distance and travel. The visitor has so many option to chose from an chose nothing as a result.

    But what is the whole site is a homepage? If the about, blogs, game and so on are all on my home page already then the visitor will feel there is more to explore. They won't have got to the bottom and this incompleteness if powerful. When I add JS to the site I will turn the top navigation bar into a progress bar to sell the effect even more.

    By the time a visitor scrolls to the bottom they will have seen the overview I wanted to give them.

    A quick not on accessibility

    reveal. The nav bar is not the first thing that visitors see. They see 'Hello World'. Screen reader users should be afforded the same experience. A screen reader reads from top to bottom and the page is designed with that in mind. When landing on the index page a screen reader should say “Hello World” and not “navigation, nav-bar”. This concept should be kept in mind throughout the coding of this site.

    What is this site?

    Considering who I am and what I want my website to achieve. What impression do I want to make and what message should the site deliver?

    Oh gosh - I hate self promotion

    A quick note before we begin. I am a firm believer of the age old writing rule: “show don't tell” (Kinda ironic that I'm telling you this).

    I find that claiming to be good at something is about as meaningful as measuring the weigh of a box by looking at it. Terms like 'good'; 'great'; 'excellent' and 'horrible' are all relative. Everyone has their own personal standard and then we get the Dunning-Kruger effect. Sure you can have something measurable like experience of qualification - but that alone doesn't necessarily imply meticulous craft, personal growth and artistic flare.

    The best way to convince someone I have the skills they want is to show them what I can do. Fortunately a portfolio is designed to do just that - yay!

    Unfortunate, when designing the portfolio I have to try and emphasise what I am good at and trick people into thinking it. In some cases I might need to claim that I am 'good' at things - without proving anything. I know it can be cringy worthy and I'll be cringing with you. But self-promotion is the aim of this game (actually it's a website) and I'd show myself to be a pretty poor designer if my system didn't do that.

    What am I doing with this website?

    It is a portfolio website. So its primary purpose is to sell a product and that product is me.

    This website will hold my portfolio as a game designer - but also as a critical and systems thinker. As a designer I tend to focus more on technical implementation and system design than art and animation. The website needs to highlight these strengths and convince the visitor that they need my services.

    I am fortunate because the skills I wish to convey are deeply linked to web-development. If this website delivers a smooth system and a good user experience, then the site itself becomes the portfolio.

    Who is me and why do you want him?

    Anyone visiting a portfolio website is aware that the purpose of the site is to impress them. The expect to see me standing in a suit and tie to show them my best-polished brass.

    Trying to impress people is not very impressive. I could be a clean cut cooperate coder, but I might also come of as a sleazy snake-oil salesman. One is dubious, and frankly the other is quite boring.

    I don't want my website to feel pretentiously professional, nor do I want it to feel like a self-pitch. I want to give it a casual vibe. I want visitors to see that I am passionate about what I am doing and feel that I am sharing that passion with them. I want them to think of this website as a dialog rather than a business pitch, a sharing ideas rather than an exhibition. Hopefully this will feel more genuine and make me seem like a creative and interesting human, rather than just another cut-out candidate.

    As an engineer-to be (if all goes according to my current plans) I have a creative edge over most technical graduates. I would have competed several humanities focused courses and engaged more deeply with the artistic and philosophical aspects of technology. Making games provides great incites into UX, UI and human centerer systems. These are useful and transferable skills when it comes to systems design an implantation.

    As an artist and designer the convers is true. I would have a professional technical degree.

    Technical creatively is my unique selling point.

    But what sets me apart form other people who have the same qualification as me? My meness. I like to think that I am driven and strive for excellence yet remain playful and love my work. (I do tend to overthinking, I'm a victim to over scoping, perfection paralysis, rambling and going off on tangents from my tangents - but that's me too.) I want the site to reflect that duality. Professional, yet playful. Clear yet creative. (Prone to random tangents and probably quite extra; yet well oiled and reliable when it matters).

    So you've waffled about yourself but what about your site?

    Simple. The site just needs to reflect everything I just said. And now the question is how.

    P.S. I don't just want the wesite to 'feel' casual, realaxed and honest. Smart people can see throught that. I want it to truly be this way. As much as it IS a portfolio website and its unavoidable primary focus is to showcase my work to potential employers, it can also be a place for me to share my work with colleagues and friends. If I'm honest, I'd love it to be the latter. Giving it elements of the later says more about me than any essey ever could.
