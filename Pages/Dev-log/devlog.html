    Re-re-reboot

    Up until quite recently most of the dev-log has been scattered across disparate document. Some of it was interesting. Some of it was hot garbage. Most of it will be lost to time.

    I'm currently in the process of adding the relevant entries here. This is a short refection of some other the prominent previous iterations of the site. (I really do faff a lot).

    So you're telling me you're just leaving half the delog out?

    Yes. Because it's irrelevant. Many of the old entries related to abandoned designs. Typing them all out would be tedious and quite unnecessary. Why should I explaining why I did what I decided not to do.

    Because it informs what comes next?

    Okay. True. It does. But I think I'll be easier if I summarise it. Instead of giving the excruciating details I am prone to provide.

    Without further ado; here are some of my abandoned ideas:

    The Sunrise

    This website was originally going to be styled to look like a sunrise… with each page based on the sky.


    When visitors first landed on my homepage, they would be greeted by a night sky. As they scrolled down, a glowing sun would rise and illuminate the page. A beautiful first impression! After that each page would have a sunrise background to continue the motif.

    This is the first website I've ever made - sunrises speak to the idea of new beginnings. An open sky invokes the idea of freedom and limitless possibilities. I also love watching the sunrise, I find it calming and relaxing.

    Furthermore, the sunrise let me use an awesome orange and blue colour scheme. The vibrant and energetic orange symbolised energy and enthusiasm while blue tempered this with a calm and trustworthy tones. Orange and blue are also contrasting colours so suing they could really make each-other pop (or clash horribly if done wrong).


    It was convoluted and the code I was using was a mess. It was becoming a pain to implement. It wasn't looking that great either. I was new to html and the site was crippled by a misshapn skeleton. It dawned on me that while the sunrise spoke to my experience of making the website it was completely unrelated to my skills as an engineer and game designer. I felt that visitors to the site would have not idea what it meant, what I did or what was going on.

    In hindsight this was not a bad idea. (I was even tempted to revert to it. The git branch for this reboot is even called 'back-to-sky'). It had the potential to be very aesthetically pleasing and was filled with a lot of good ideas.

    The 'hello' world into screen and final colour scheme were both cannibalised form this iteration. As was my choice to use simple and readable body text.

    Star gazing

    This was a haphazard branch of the sunrise theme, originally intended to be the design form my dev-log. I had the idea of arranging elements on my website in constellations. I wanted exploring the site to feel like looking though the night sky invoking awe and wonder.


    Setting something like this up is hard and I was trying to show off with skills I didn't have. (Funny enough I think I could pull it off now, but I know I shouldn't). This iteration had all the bad parts of the sky one. The design rationale was essentially: “it looks cool, and I like space” The consultations were messy and hard to code. I stopped working on the site and lost motivation.

    Bluprints

    An ironic theme for a failed plan. This time round the idea had reason and logic to it. Blueprints speak to creativity and design. They reflect technical capability and look awful.


    To be fair they could've looked good, but I didn't have a defined style guid, just a vague idea. As a result, I potted around with 'blue-print-like' styling. I wanted the site to look cutting edge but instead it looked like it came from the 90's.


    Again, not a bad idea, but horrid execution. Even now I am shuddering. My mind is scarred with 'error screen blue'.

    The wireframes for this iteration however where great and I recycled some of their concepts for the final build. You may recognise the dev-log timeline.

    Rainbows

    This was the next notable idea I had, and it formed the framework for the site you are on right now. I'd begun to realize a pattern with my failed sites. The ideas were great, the rational was decent and the implementation was hideous. Maybe my ideas were too big. Maybe I was giving up to early. Maybe I wasn't focused. Most likely it was all the above.

    A lot of the style and structure details for rainbows have been implemented here, so you'll see plenty abridged versions of my rambling in this log. Rainbows was less about styling and more about a philosophy.

    This time round I was focused on what the site said and how it felt, rather than how it looked. I tried to be minimal in my approach to avoid technical pit falls and I planned less and did more.

    All my previous work had been subject to meticulous planning and over-design. I spent hours saying what I was going to do and then doing nothing. Rainbows meant to be more hands on. I defined a core theme and style and then I was to set about building a page that would fit it. Little by little, line by line.

    Importunely I'm still prone to massive deliberation. CSS is a deep rabbit-hole and sematic markup is a bottomless pit. I stubbled down both on my occasion along with an inability to make simple design choices without 5000 revisions.

    I still need to learn how to move on and say good enough is good enough. But I am leaning, and I will get there.


    The only major feature I dropped from rainbows was the colour-scheme. I used all colours of the rainbow to make the site look vibrant and colourful. This destroyed all legibility and compositional unity. All the colours were just a bit too much.

    Going back to the 'back' button.

    The back button was never well placed on my wireframes and has always felt like an award appendage cluttering up the site. I'm still not 100% sure where to put it, but at least I've thought things through.

    What doess the back button do?

    The back button allows the user to go back to where they game from. I'm renaming it the return button because the fits the “programming” theme better. (Return is the command given to leave a function).

    What's the issue with it?

    The back buttons on the blog and dev-log pages are a bit of a mess. They float around haphazardly feeling like clutter wherever they go.

    I've moved most navigation and index features to the right, which is great, but it means that there is even less space for the button to naturally rest.

    I was considering putting it on the nav bar - but I want the nave bar to have the same fields throughout the site to give a sense of consistency. The nav-bar is also quite cluttered as it is.

    Can't the user just navigate with the nav bar? Why is return so important?

    Currently it's not doing that much. But once I start adding some JS the return button will send the user back to their previous page, wherever that was. This makes navigating the site easier (which is never a bad thing). So, no. No, I do not want to do away with the return button.

    To be honest I'm not sure where it should go. I could throw it onto the right-hand side, but it would look weird and lonely there. Besides, the website is in English which sets a natural left-to right convention. Returning or going back can be seen as leftwards movement, so putting a return button on the right may seem unintuitive.

    I'm going to be reformatting it to look like a piece of code and throw it under the title. I'll pop it again at the bottom of the article. Logically these are the two points where a reader is most likely to decide they want to go back. Either they haven't started and decide they'd rather look at something else, or they finished reading and want to go back.

    With this in mind I'll probably be floating a return button above the index section and again at the bottom of the article. Being above the index will put the button in the comfortable top left corner - where one expects a back button to be. The one at the bottom of the page let's users return when they are finished reading. Is this overkill? Perhaps a back at the end is goo enough. Is there a point in asking someone to leave when they just arrived?

    Possibly. I'll have to wait and see. I think I'll throw it in at below the index (at least for now). This groups it with naiation which seems logical. I can always re-move it later…

    Pseudo square brackets: what, how, why?


    Square brackets have been a recurring motif on my sight because they invoke ideas of theology and programming.
    (They also look cool!)
    . But using inline text elements to represent them is tedious and creates clutter for screen reader users: there must be a better way.

    Why do you want these square brackets anyway?

    Decoration. This site isn't exactly putting on a sceptical with pretty images and fancy graphics. Of course, this is by design - but having no decoration at all is boring. The brackets add a bit of intrigue and variety while acting as a motif. They give the site a distinct aesthetic style with should make it more memorable to visitors. Furthermore, it ensures that all pages on the site (I know, this sounds like a horribly mixed metaphor…) are look like parts of a common whole; even when their layout and content is varied.

    Okay, cool. But why use square brackets for this?

    Decoration. This site isn't exactly putting on a sceptical with pretty images and fancy graphics. Of course, this is by design - but having no decoration at all is boring. The brackets add a bit of intrigue and variety while acting as a motif. They give the site a distinct aesthetic style with should make it more memorable to visitors. Furthermore, it ensures that all pages on the site (I know, this sounds like a horribly mixed metaphor…) are look like parts of a common whole; even when their layout and content is varied.

    So? Why not are semi colons, ampersands, or OTHER BRACKETS - these are used in code too!

    Well, yes. But square ones are the best! (In my case anyway).

    Firstly, square brackets are often used for indexing in programming languages. And this is how I am using brackets on my site. They indicate tilts and section: items that would appear in and index. If my site were some sort of dictionary class, this is exactly how a under might expect to retrieve the section associated with a title. Tying a functional meaning to the elements is important because it reinforces the connection to programming (and shows that I know what I'm doing).

    Other coding syntax elements lack his meaning. Semi-colons are just line endings and curly brackets often just contain sections of code. (As for good old round brackets, their meaning is already well defined as an element of English punctuation. Not not just meaningless, they're but confusing too!)

    I also like square brackets because they appear strong and confident. Most containers on this site have crisp, sharp boarders with unrounded edges - square braces fit comfortably into this astatic. (Although I must confess square braces may have also informed this athletic)

    So what's wrong with them?

    Look at these two random containers:
    [Something in brackets]
    Something in brackets

    Is this a trick or something?Ones one is on top the other is on the bottom?

    If you are viewing this site on a monitor, they probably look the same. But if you view them with a screen reader one is haven then the other is hell. (And not becuase of that top and bottom thing.)

    Is this a trick or something?Ones one is on top the other is on the bottom?

    Open square bracket imagine close square bracket if I spoke like open square brace this close square brace.

    I can't even understand what you are saying.

    Exactly! That's what happens if decorative square brackets are added as inline characters. Like I did with the first div (and most of the early headers on this site).

    Take a look at the code for the first one to see what I mean: 

                        
  <style>
    .square-bracket-bad {
      font-weight: bold;
      color: rgb(47, 177, 203);
    }
    .square-bracket-bad .inner {
      font-weight: normal;
      color: black;
      margin: 0 10px;
    }
  </style>

  <div class="square-bracket-bad">
    [<span class="inner">Something in brackets</span>]
  </div>

    Oh dear. That's a bummer... I'm guessing the second div is different...

    How do you fix this>

    Magic. (Actually CSS, which is basically the same thing.)

    I made used of the ::before and ::after CSS pseudo elements. These elements render (you'll never guess this) before and after their parent element. Think of them like the bullet points on a list. They are pretty neat and simple to use.

    Just look at the code for the lower div:

                        
  <style>
   .square-bracket-good {
      font-weight: normal;
      color: black;
    }
    .square-bracket-good::before {
      content: "[";
      font-weight: bold;
      color: rgb(47, 177, 203);
      margin-right: 10px;
    }
    .square-bracket-good::after {
     content: "]";
     font-weight: bold;
     color: rgb(47, 177, 203);
     margin-left: 10px;
  }
  </style>

  <div class="square-bracket-good">Something in brackets</div>

    Did you notice the lack of braces? Well screen reader users noticed that too! So did web crawlers and anything else that is interfacing directly with the code on my site. Removing square brackets and other decretive clutter makes the code more sematic and machine readable.

    Pseudo elements aren't real. They can't hurt you. (Pseudo literally means fake!) These elements are defined entirely with scc styling which means that, like all good children in the Middle Ages, they can be seen but not heard.

    Another great thing about the pseudo element approach is the flexibility it gives me. Let's say I get hooked on C++ and decide that titles aren't really indexes _ there're pointers. So, I want to change all of the []'s into *'s. If I need to change the characters next to every single heading, I'd probably give up or pass out from boredom. Fortunately, with pseudo elements this is as simple as changing two lines of CSS . If the style s applied to a class, this can allow innumerable html elements to be reformatted.

    Done! Now I must go get rid of all those legacy brackets on my site. (Or write a program to do it for me). Wish me luck!

    What happens when you land on the homepage?

    Taking a look at how a visitor may navigate though the site and finding ways to enhance that experience.

    What is important about the homepage?

    The homepage has to make a good impression. It is likely the first (and possibly only) thing with visitors to my site will see.

    It needs to amaze them, interest them and make them want to see more! I may not have long to get a visitators attention. Visors must be enthralled but they should be able to immediately know what I do when they glace at my webpage.

    How will you do this?

    I have a couple tricks up my sleeve…

    'Hello World.'

    That will be the first message visitors see when they land on my site. I know it's quite cliched, however it says a lot. When learning to program in a new language, printing 'hello world' is one of the first programs one writes. Opening with 'hello world' immediately tells the visitor - 'Hey I'm a programmer. I made this.

    "Hellow World" is also a greeting to the world. I'm acknowledging that I have just gone live on the internet for the first time. And that's pretty cool! This grounds also grounds me: it acknowledges that I have just begun my journey as a developer and still need to lean and grow while alos commemerating that fact that I am live on the wrold wide web.

    The visitor is just greeted by a back screen with 'Hello world' on it. It should feel like the command consol. Empty and blank. A monospace font is used to enhance this effect.


    And then what?


    They then scroll down and the site is revealed. The emergence of a bright and polished sight should have a significant impact. Everything will look brighter and slicker in contrast to the first back screen. This is to make and impactful first impression and convey the idea of personal growth.

    A rounded san-serif font will be used to enhance this contrast.

    Just Keep Scrowling

    As the visitor scrolls they unveil the site. In due course JavaScript will help me make this experience feel exciting and polished. This will transform the mundane act of scrolling down a webpage into an adventure. As the viewer scrolls down the site they become curios to see how things will change and what will come next. While scrolling, I introduce myself and I invite the visitor to look around the site. I don't necessarily expect them to follow my prompts. I just want them to keep scrolling.

    Once hey have started so they'll probably continue. I don't expect victors to explore my whole site (even if I would like them to) they'll probably just look at the homepage. Following hyperlinks implies distance and travel. The visitor has so many option to chose from an chose nothing as a result.

    But what is the whole site is a homepage? If the about, blogs, game and so on are all on my home page already then the visitor will feel there is more to explore. They won't have got to the bottom and this incompleteness if powerful. When I add JS to the site I will turn the top navigation bar into a progress bar to sell the effect even more.

    By the time a visitor scrolls to the bottom they will have seen the overview I wanted to give them.

    What is this site?

    Considering who I am and what I want my website to achieve. What impression do I want to make and what message should the site deliver?

    Oh gosh - I hate self promotion

    A quick note before we begin. I am a firm believer of the age old writing rule: “show don't tell” (Kinda ironic that I'm telling you this).

    I find that claiming to be good at something is about as meaningful as measuring the weigh of a box by looking at it. Terms like 'good'; 'great'; 'excellent' and 'horrible' are all relative. Everyone has their own personal standard and then we get the Dunning-Kruger effect. Sure you can have something measurable like experience of qualification - but that alone doesn't necessarily imply meticulous craft, personal growth and artistic flare.

    The best way to convince someone I have the skills they want is to show them what I can do. Fortunately a portfolio is designed to do just that - yay!

    Unfortunate, when designing the portfolio I have to try and emphasise what I am good at and trick people into thinking it. In some cases I might need to claim that I am 'good' at things - without proving anything. I know it can be cringy worthy and I'll be cringing with you. But self-promotion is the aim of this game (actually it's a website) and I'd show myself to be a pretty poor designer if my system didn't do that.

    What am I doing with this website?

    It is a portfolio website. So its primary purpose is to sell a product and that product is me.

    This website will hold my portfolio as a game designer - but also as a critical and systems thinker. As a designer I tend to focus more on technical implementation and system design than art and animation. The website needs to highlight these strengths and convince the visitor that they need my services.

    I am fortunate because the skills I wish to convey are deeply linked to web-development. If this website delivers a smooth system and a good user experience, then the site itself becomes the portfolio.

    Who is me and why do you want him?

    Anyone visiting a portfolio website is aware that the purpose of the site is to impress them. The expect to see me standing in a suit and tie to show them my best-polished brass.

    Trying to impress people is not very impressive. I could be a clean cut cooperate coder, but I might also come of as a sleazy snake-oil salesman. One is dubious, and frankly the other is quite boring.

    I don't want my website to feel pretentiously professional, nor do I want it to feel like a self-pitch. I want to give it a casual vibe. I want visitors to see that I am passionate about what I am doing and feel that I am sharing that passion with them. I want them to think of this website as a dialog rather than a business pitch, a sharing ideas rather than an exhibition. Hopefully this will feel more genuine and make me seem like a creative and interesting human, rather than just another cut-out candidate.

    As an engineer-to be (if all goes according to my current plans) I have a creative edge over most technical graduates. I would have competed several humanities focused courses and engaged more deeply with the artistic and philosophical aspects of technology. Making games provides great incites into UX, UI and human centerer systems. These are useful and transferable skills when it comes to systems design an implantation.

    As an artist and designer the convers is true. I would have a professional technical degree.

    Technical creatively is my unique selling point.

    But what sets me apart form other people who have the same qualification as me? My meness. I like to think that I am driven and strive for excellence yet remain playful and love my work. (I do tend to overthinking, I'm a victim to over scoping, perfection paralysis, rambling and going off on tangents from my tangents - but that's me too.) I want the site to reflect that duality. Professional, yet playful. Clear yet creative. (Prone to random tangents and probably quite extra; yet well oiled and reliable when it matters).

    So you've waffled about yourself but what about your site?

    Simple. The site just needs to reflect everything I just said. And now the question is how.
